# Process Log 20260419_221911

This log explains the Prompt 4 manual pilot concepts for a beginner.

## Pilot Ingestion Run

What it is: a small controlled trial that moves candidate image references through collection, copy/download, filtering, and review queue creation.

Why it is used here: TyreVisionX needs to prove the tyre-specific curation loop before scaling.

Why not another approach yet: scaling directly to thousands of images would make errors harder to debug.

How it is implemented: `scripts/web_collection/run_manual_pilot.py` calls `src/web_collection/pilot.py` and writes pilot outputs under `data/interim/web_candidates/pilot_01/`.

## Candidate Metadata

What it is: structured information about each image candidate.

Why it is used here: every candidate needs traceable source, query, file, quality, triage, and review fields.

Why not another approach yet: loose folders of images would lose provenance.

How it is implemented: `CandidateRecord` and CSV outputs contain source URLs, local paths, hashes, quality status, anomaly status, and review status.

## Provenance Tracking

What it is: keeping the source history of each candidate.

Why it is used here: web images require source URL/page URL tracking for research integrity and rights review.

Why not another approach yet: deleting provenance would make future audits impossible.

How it is implemented: the pilot template and metadata keep `source_url`, `page_url`, `query_text`, and `local_source_path`.

## Manual URL CSV

What it is: a researcher-approved spreadsheet of image candidates.

Why it is used here: it avoids unauthorized scraping and gives the user control over the pilot source list.

Why not another approach yet: provider APIs can be added later after credentials and policy decisions.

How it is implemented: `data/external/manual_candidate_urls/approved_tyres_pilot_urls_template.csv` defines the schema.

## Local File-Path Ingestion

What it is: using local files as candidate sources instead of web URLs.

Why it is used here: it allows offline controlled testing.

Why not another approach yet: network access and rights-approved sources may not be ready.

How it is implemented: `local_source_path` rows are converted to `file://` source URLs and copied into pilot raw outputs.

## Exact Hash Vs Perceptual Hash

What it is: exact hash detects byte-identical files; perceptual hash detects visually similar images.

Why it is used here: web images often contain duplicates or resized copies.

Why not another approach yet: learned duplicate detection is more complex than needed for the first pilot.

How it is implemented: `src/web_collection/filters.py` computes SHA-256 and average perceptual hashes.

## Hard Filter Vs Soft Review Flag

What it is: hard filters reject unusable candidates; soft flags send questionable candidates to review.

Why it is used here: the project should avoid bad data while not over-discarding edge cases.

Why not another approach yet: automatic high-confidence decisions would be risky at this stage.

How it is implemented: filtering creates kept, rejected, and review-needed CSVs.

## Review Queue

What it is: a table of candidates ready for human review.

Why it is used here: human review is the gate before promotion to likely-normal data.

Why not another approach yet: anomaly or quality scores are not labels.

How it is implemented: `build_review_queue` and pilot reports create queue CSVs with `pending_review` statuses.

## Review Pack

What it is: a visual package such as a contact sheet and gallery for reviewers.

Why it is used here: reviewing images is easier when thumbnails and metadata are bundled together.

Why not another approach yet: a full review web app would be overkill for a first pilot.

How it is implemented: `scripts/web_collection/export_review_pack.py` creates a contact-sheet PNG and markdown gallery when local images exist.

## Reviewed Decision File

What it is: the CSV where final human decisions are recorded.

Why it is used here: promotion must be based on explicit human review.

Why not another approach yet: implicit approvals or model-only labels would contaminate the data.

How it is implemented: `reports/web_collection/pilot_review_decisions_template.csv` is the decision template.

## Curated Likely-Normal Manifest

What it is: a manifest of human-approved likely-normal web candidates.

Why it is used here: anomaly training can later expand beyond the original good tyre images.

Why not another approach yet: no manifest should be created from unreviewed data.

How it is implemented: `promote_reviewed_candidates.py` writes `data/manifests/web_curated_tyres_likely_normal_v1.csv` only for `approved_likely_normal` rows.

## Curated Web Candidates Are Not Benchmark Labels

What it is: reviewed web candidates are not the same as controlled benchmark dataset labels.

Why it is used here: provenance and review process differ from D1/D2/D3 datasets.

Why not another approach yet: mixing them silently would make evaluation claims unclear.

How it is implemented: curated web rows use `source_dataset = web_curated_pilot_01` and `label_str = likely_normal_web_reviewed`.

## Tyre-First Before Generalization

What it is: focusing the immediate pipeline on tyre anomaly readiness.

Why it is used here: the research project needs a strong tyre-specific result first.

Why not another approach yet: broad platform work would dilute the current research goal.

How it is implemented: docs, reports, and prompts emphasize tyre candidate collection and tyre likely-normal promotion.

## Why A Pilot Matters Before Scaling

What it is: a small test run before collecting thousands of images.

Why it is used here: it verifies schema, provenance, filtering, review, and promotion logic.

Why not another approach yet: scaling broken data workflows creates expensive cleanup later.

How it is implemented: the pilot is capped at 20-50 intended candidates and reports blocked state if input is missing.
