# Process Log 20260419_231704

## False Negatives In Anomaly Detection

What it is: a false negative is a defective tyre that the anomaly model predicts as normal.

Why it is used here: TyreVisionX is recall-critical, so missed defects are the main risk.

Why not another approach yet: before adding methods, the project must understand what the current model misses.

How it is implemented: `scripts/anomaly/analyze_false_negatives.py` summarizes and visualizes `false_negatives_test.csv`.

## Global Vs Local Features

What it is: global features describe the whole image; local features focus on patches or regions.

Why it is used here: small tyre cracks may be diluted in a whole-image embedding.

Why not another approach yet: global ResNet embeddings are a simple first baseline.

How it is implemented: reports explain that pooled ResNet18 embeddings may miss local defects.

## Mahalanobis Vs kNN

What it is: Mahalanobis uses a global normal distribution; kNN uses distance to nearby normal examples.

Why it is used here: kNN may behave differently when normal data is multi-modal.

Why not another approach yet: both are simple and compatible with existing embeddings.

How it is implemented: `anomaly_knn.yaml` defines the next planned scorer comparison.

## Threshold Sweeps

What it is: trying different decision thresholds to trade recall against false positives.

Why it is used here: the current threshold controls normal FPR but misses many defects.

Why not another approach yet: threshold analysis should precede new complex models.

How it is implemented: `anomaly_threshold_sweep.yaml` defines validation-only sweep rules.

## Pseudo-Labeling Scraped Images Is Risky

What it is: pseudo-labeling means treating model guesses as labels.

Why it matters here: the current anomaly model has low recall, and web images are noisy.

Why not another approach yet: using guessed labels can contaminate training data.

How it is implemented: safe web-normal policy forbids auto-labeling and pseudo-labeled retraining.

## Dataset Contamination In Anomaly Learning

What it is: contamination happens when abnormal or unreliable images enter the normal training set.

Why it is used here: anomaly models learn what normal means; contaminated normals make defects look normal.

Why not another approach yet: strict review is safer than automated expansion.

How it is implemented: only human-approved likely-normal candidates may enter curated manifests.

## Model-Assisted Triage Vs Labeling

What it is: triage ranks candidates for review; labeling decides ground truth.

Why it is used here: model scores can help prioritize, but humans must decide.

Why not another approach yet: model-only decisions are not reliable enough.

How it is implemented: `src/web_collection/confidence.py` creates review-priority buckets, not labels.

## Tyre-First Before Platform Generalization

What it is: focusing implementation on tyre anomaly readiness before broad manufacturing abstractions.

Why it is used here: the research milestone is a credible tyre workflow.

Why not another approach yet: platform generalization is premature if tyre performance is weak.

How it is implemented: this prompt added tyre anomaly analysis and safe tyre web-normal policy, not a platform rewrite.
