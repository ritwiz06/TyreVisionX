## Manual Google Discovery Pack

What it is:
A manual discovery pack is a set of browser-ready search queries, a checklist, and a prefilled CSV that helps a researcher collect candidate image sources consistently.

Why it is used here:
TyreVisionX needs the first 30-50 likely-normal tyre web candidates, but the process must be controlled and auditable.

Why not another approach yet:
Automated scraping would be faster but unsafe, less reproducible, and explicitly forbidden for Google.

How it is implemented in TyreVisionX:
`scripts/web_collection/generate_manual_google_discovery_pack.py` generates markdown query batches, a checklist, a prefilled CSV, and a notebook.

## Browser-Ready Query

What it is:
A browser-ready query is a clickable Google Images search URL prepared for manual use.

Why it is used here:
It saves the researcher from typing queries and keeps the first pilot aligned with the planned tyre-view groups.

Why not another approach yet:
The project is not ready for automated provider ingestion at scale; first it needs a small clean pilot.

How it is implemented in TyreVisionX:
`reports/web_collection/manual_google_query_batches.md` contains 40 links grouped by tread, sidewall, mounted tyre, inspection-like, and industrial/off-highway views.

## Negative Keywords

What it is:
Negative keywords are search terms prefixed with a minus sign to reduce unwanted results.

Why it is used here:
The first pilot is looking for likely-normal tyre candidates, so words like damage, cracked, puncture, burst, and defect are excluded from search queries.

Why not another approach yet:
Negative keywords are only a weak filter. They reduce noise but cannot guarantee normal images.

How it is implemented in TyreVisionX:
Every generated Google query includes damage/defect negative keywords while still requiring manual review.

## Prefilled Candidate CSV

What it is:
A prefilled CSV is a spreadsheet-like file with candidate IDs, query IDs, query text, and blank columns for the researcher to complete.

Why it is used here:
It prevents inconsistent manual notes and makes later import easier.

Why not another approach yet:
Free-form notes alone would be hard to parse and audit.

How it is implemented in TyreVisionX:
`data/external/manual_candidate_urls/approved_tyres_pilot_urls_001_prefilled.csv` contains 40 rows with blank `source_url`, `page_url`, `local_source_path`, and `notes` fields.

## Candidate Versus Label

What it is:
A candidate is an image that may be useful after review. A label is a trusted training target.

Why it is used here:
Web search results can look normal but still be defective, irrelevant, low quality, or legally unsuitable.

Why not another approach yet:
Auto-labeling web images as good would contaminate the anomaly training set.

How it is implemented in TyreVisionX:
The pack never assigns `good` or `defect`; it only prepares rows for later filtering and review.

## Selection Rubric

What it is:
A rubric is a checklist of approval and rejection criteria.

Why it is used here:
It helps the researcher make consistent decisions while browsing tyre images.

Why not another approach yet:
Without a rubric, the pilot could mix normal tyres, damaged tyres, renders, and irrelevant images.

How it is implemented in TyreVisionX:
`reports/web_collection/manual_candidate_selection_rubric.md` explains what to approve, what to avoid, how to record license/source notes, and why candidates are not labels.

## Pilot-First Data Growth

What it is:
Pilot-first data growth means collecting a small, inspected batch before scaling.

Why it is used here:
The project needs to prove the collection, filtering, review, and promotion workflow before collecting thousands of images.

Why not another approach yet:
Scaling a flawed workflow would create a large noisy dataset.

How it is implemented in TyreVisionX:
The generated pack targets 40 rows, which fits the planned 30-50 candidate pilot.
