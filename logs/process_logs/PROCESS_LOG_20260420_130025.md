# Process Log 20260420_130025

## Multi-Crop Inference
What it is: run the same model on the full image and several crops.

Why it is used here: a tyre crack may appear in one area, so a crop may make the defect more visible than the whole image.

Why not another approach yet: full patch-anomaly frameworks are heavier; multicrop is simple and explainable.

How TyreVisionX implements it: `src/anomaly/multicrop.py` creates full, corner, and center crops. `resnet50_multicrop_knn` scores all crops and uses the maximum score as the image anomaly score.

## Patch-Grid Embeddings
What it is: split the image into a grid and score each patch.

Why it is used here: local tyre damage can be small, and grid patches test whether smaller regions carry stronger anomaly evidence.

Why not another approach yet: this is a first controlled local-feature test before a heavier PatchCore-style memory bank.

How TyreVisionX implements it: `resnet50_patch_grid_knn_fine` uses the full image plus a 3x3 patch grid.

## Multi-Scale Anomaly Scoring
What it is: combine anomaly evidence from different view sizes.

Why it is used here: tyre defects may be visible at full-image, crop, or patch scale.

Why not another approach yet: this prompt focused on two simple local scales first.

How TyreVisionX implements it: full-image reference, multicrop, and patch-grid variants are compared in one benchmark table.

## Local-Feature Aggregation
What it is: combine multiple crop/patch scores into one image-level score.

Why it is used here: the final benchmark still needs one normal/anomaly decision per tyre image.

Why not another approach yet: max aggregation is conservative and easy to audit.

How TyreVisionX implements it: `src/anomaly/local_features.py` supports max, top-2 mean, and mean aggregation. The benchmark used max aggregation.

## Validation-Only Threshold Sweep
What it is: choose a decision threshold using validation data only.

Why it is used here: TyreVisionX is recall-critical, so thresholding can reduce false negatives without retraining.

Why not another approach yet: tuning on test would invalidate the comparison.

How TyreVisionX implements it: `resnet50_knn_threshold_sweep` picks a validation threshold with higher allowed normal false-positive rate, then applies it once to test.

## False-Negative Reduction
What it is: reducing the number of defective tyres predicted as normal.

Why it is used here: false negatives are the highest-risk failure in tyre inspection.

Why not optimize accuracy first: accuracy can hide dangerous misses when recall is the priority.

How TyreVisionX measures it: every variant reports test recall and false-negative count in `local_feature_model_comparison.csv`.

## Why ResNet50 Is the Current Base Model
What it is: a larger pretrained CNN backbone than ResNet18.

Why it is used here: previous benchmarks showed ResNet50+kNN was the strongest available tyre anomaly variant.

Why not jump to bigger models: larger weights were not the focus, and local evidence was the current hypothesis.

How TyreVisionX implements it: local ResNet50 ImageNet weights are loaded from `artifacts/.torch/hub/checkpoints/resnet50-11ad3fa6.pth`.

## Why Noise Robustness Is Secondary Now
What it is: improving stability under blur, noise, compression, or lighting changes.

Why it matters: inspection images can vary.

Why it is secondary here: robustness work did not reduce the main clean false-negative issue.

How TyreVisionX uses the finding: robustness remains useful, but the current priority is recall and false-negative reduction.

## Why Local Defect Evidence Matters for Tyres
What it is: defects such as cracks can be small and regional.

Why it is used here: whole-image pooling can dilute small evidence.

Why not assume local methods will win: naive patches can create distribution mismatch and fail, as seen in the fine patch-grid result.

How TyreVisionX tests it: local variants are compared honestly against the reference and threshold sweep.

## Why Bigger Models Are Not Automatically Next
What it is: larger backbones can have more representation capacity.

Why not use them blindly: a bigger global model may still miss small local defects and may require unavailable local weights.

How TyreVisionX handles this: it first tests ResNet50 local evidence and records results before moving to heavier methods.
